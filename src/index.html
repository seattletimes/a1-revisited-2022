What we found when we examined our 1942 coverage of Executive Order 9066

By Crystal Paul and Emily M. Eng

Seattle Times editors

Project Editor’s Note:

In February 1942, a few short months after the attack at Pearl Harbor, President Franklin D. Roosevelt signed Executive Order 9066 designating Japanese Americans as “threats” to national security and requiring them to leave their homes on the West Coast to be incarcerated at concentration camps.

On March 30, 1942, Bainbridge Island became the first community where executive order 9066 was fully enforced and citizens and residents were forcibly removed from their homes and incarcerated. The Seattle Times (then known as The Seattle Daily Times) covered the events of that day in 1942 in three stories on the A1 (the front page) and A2 pages of our March 30 issue.

Eighty 80 years later, in an effort to take accountability for and learn from the harm caused by our coverage that day, The Times has revisited the stories, photos and headlines that told the story of that historic event in our pages.

We formed a team of journalists, editors, designers, news researchers and photo editors, and collaborated with local non-profit organization Densho, to scrutinize every word, caption, photo and design decision made in telling those stories. In our examination, we found that our 1942 coverage used racist slurs and stereotypes, inaccurate and offensive language, one-sided sourcing and framing perspective, and design and photo decisions photos and layouts that downplayed the significance of the events of that day.

We compiled these observations into notes on the 1942 pages, highlighting where each issue appears, then we categorized the issues identified and added these as tags in the annotations. Here, you will see the most glaring examples in each category.

In an effort to avoid further harm, we chose to remove the racist slurs in this print edition, but, in the interest of historical accuracy, you can find the original pages in our archive and in our online presentation where readers can opt-in to see the pages without those removals.

Our annotations identify the specific issues in our 1942 coverage and compare them to how we would cover this topic today. What we found highlights stark differences in our journalistic standards in 1942 versus today. We regret the enduring legacy of the racist practices documented in these pages, and we also acknowledge that racism is not a thing of the past, but we know The Seattle Times has continuing work to do as we aspire to provide accurate, sensitive, and inclusive coverage for all of our communities. This project has provided valuable insights that we hope will continue to inform and strengthen our journalism.

— Crystal Paul, project editor
